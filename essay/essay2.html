the world of digital colonialism and human labour

In todays world, we as people have utilised the benefits of technology alongside each new wave of technological advancement. We are a generation of people who are proudly exclaiming that we no longer have to work twice as hard as our parents and grandparents, but rather we comfortable in our work spaces and have allowed the machines to do the majority of the heavy lifting. There is pride in knowing that we have new emerging tech giants who are constantly figuring out new ways to make our lives easier because at the end of the day that is what technology is, a tool to make our lives easier. With this achievement however, there are bound to be some downsides that may or may not shatter the illusion of the perfect tool. One of those downsides has created a large problem concerning employment and human labour being replaced by technological tools like AI. And we are now stuck asking the infamous question of many who have faced life changing dilemmas in their darkest of times, “What Now?”.

In this essay, I will dive into the world of digital colonialism and human labour. This essay aims to shed light on the increasing problems within the workplace that faced by many who work and handle technology and navigate the digital space on behalf of their employers. Companies are now laying off majority of their staff in favour of the newly developing, ever expanding, knowledge-finding tools associated with Artificial Intelligence. To back up this proposed issue, I will be using Sareeta Amruta’s speech on "Tech Colonialism Today" delivered at the EPIC2019 conference on the EPIC (Ethnographic Praxis in Industry Conference) website. I will also be utilising an article on CNBC titled “Google lays off hundreds of ‘Core’ employees, moves some positions to India and Mexico” by Jennifer Elias. This article will be used as a real life example of these emerging issues in the workspace. Additional research will be conducted and articles will be referenced throughout this essay.

KEY WORDS: Digital Colonialism, Artificial Intelligence

DIGITAL COLONIALISM AND THE NEGATIVE EFFECT ON TECH WORKERS

According to the Boston Review, Digital colonialism is basically the control of digital and technological infrastructure by a few powerful Western corporations. This modern form of colonialism not only has control over data, but also communication networks, and digital economies. Unlike traditional colonialism, which involved the abuse and exploitation of physical territories, digital colonialism influences how information is accessed, processed, and shared (2023).

In my understanding from the above text, digital colonialism is essentially huge companies trying to monopolise the market and creating cheap labour through the use of new technology that can get the work done by cutting down half the time and half the labour force. These companies seek to use this as a way of saving major costs while returning big profits. This in turn adds onto the growing rate of unemployment and is essentially terrible for the economy overall but great for the companies.

An example of this is the article published by J. Elias that details the digital colonisation that is currently occurring at Google. Google, the very popular and well known tech company popularised by the search engine feature and huge data return, has laid off employees in the west and relocating its business to the Eastern countries where labour is cheap (2024). According to Elias, Google has already laid off 200 of their core employees and is now relocating those positions to India and Mexico whereby the same amount of work and roles will be given but maybe at a cheaper cost than their initial employees who have now grown used to the average price a western employee would make at a tech company (2024).

Elias is also stating that google is also stating that this major transformation is due to making its developer tools simpler while adding more AI features. Some teams like governance and protected data are being reduced, likely due to more rules and regulations. Google leaders say that advances in AI are causing these changes, calling it a big turning point. People losing their jobs can try to find other jobs within Google and get help with finding jobs elsewhere. Google wants to focus more on important products by “moving resources around” which in my understanding is trying to figure out how to achieve the objective without spending too much (2024).

DIGITAL COLONIALISM IN AFRICA

As much as I would hope this would only stop with google, there is an unfortunate reality that other major companies are utilising digital Colonialism. Worst of all, these companies are now pushing digital colonisation towards Africa.

Another example would be the very popular social media app called Facebook, founded by Mark Zuckerburg and owned by the Meta franchise. In a 2021 article by Bigtech, it was stated that Zuckerberg is engaging in digital colonialism through initiatives like Free Basics and major infrastructure investments in Africa and India. Free Basics offers limited free internet access, making Facebook the main gateway to the internet. This would make Facebook a primary internet provider of which it will compete with other local internet providers. In Africa, Facebook's $1 billion 2Africa undersea cable project raises concerns about control over digital infrastructure, while in India, Free Basics was banned due to net neutrality issues, though Facebook continues to seek influence in these regions (BigTechtopia, 2023).

As you can see, these western companies have already planned for their “invasion” into the untapped digital market of African countries. South Africa alone may be one of the few technologically advanced African countries, but in comparison to the rest of the world, we are still leagues behind, which for us is unfortunate seeing as how there is a major gap to cover but very fortunate for emerging tech giants who plan to utilise this underdevelopment in their favour. South Africa has just entered the digital world but already there is a large competition between cheap foreign labour and Artificial Intelligence to worry about.

And with all that is happening, we still have more digital “apartheid” based companies that are utilising this technology to further push racial agendas in South Africa. According to MIT Technology review, a local security company called Vumacam has partnered with international companies from the North to provide additional security with regards to cameras. Unfortunately, this has resulted in the company pushing racial agendas by creating divide between areas whereby the more predominantly black areas are targeted and the more upper class areas with a white majority are well protected and guarded (2023)

These instances are doing more harm than good, and it really is giving these major companies a bad look with regards to the migration and integration into less developed ares.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, while technology has significantly improved our lives by reducing the need for manual labor and increasing efficiency, it also poses serious challenges. Digital colonialism is one such challenge, where powerful Western tech companies dominate global digital infrastructure and labor markets. This dominance leads to job losses and economic dependencies in less developed regions. For example, companies like Google and Facebook are shifting operations to countries like India and Africa, leveraging cheaper labor and minimal regulations. This trend not only exacerbates unemployment in Western countries but also imposes control over the digital landscape of developing nations, undermining local economies and innovation. Addressing these issues requires a balanced approach that ensures technological advancement benefits all, without exacerbating inequalities or creating new forms of exploitation.
